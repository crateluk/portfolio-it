Pop Tale

Pop Tale è un progetto di tesi di gruppo sviluppato in quasi un anno da un team di sei studenti. Questo cortometraggio animato mi ha dato l’opportunità di crescere sia dal punto di vista tecnico che personale, lavorando all’interno di una pipeline di produzione completa, dal concept al render finale. Le mie principali responsabilità hanno riguardato gli effetti visivi, il rendering e il compositing, ma ho anche fornito supporto nella modellazione 3D, contribuendo alla creazione di asset e ambienti.
Oltre agli aspetti tecnici, Pop Tale è stata un’esperienza formativa in termini di lavoro di squadra, comunicazione e problem-solving, che mi ha permesso di sviluppare un approccio collaborativo, e la capacità di adattarmi alle sfide creative e tecniche lungo tutto il processo di produzione.

 

 

Project overview

 

What if the solution to your problems came from a commercial? How likely would it be a scam? Chester Wyatt never really asked himself that question… or maybe he did, just a little too late. Chester, a lazy and disorganized farmer,  suddenly finds himself caught in a challenge far beyond his abilities: turning his long-neglected cornfield into the best in the county, all to win a tempting cash prize. And he has less than twenty-four hours to do it. A hopeless task… or maybe there’s still a chance.
Pop Tale was born from the clear intention to create something genuinely fun — a story capable of making people smile while drawing them into a carefully crafted world. From this simple yet effective idea began our creative adventure: a challenge to ourselves, a long and meticulous journey built on artistic research and strong teamwork. Every visual choice, every animation, every detail of the environments and characters was the result of collective discussion, sometimes heated, but always constructive.

 

CHARACTERS

Chester Wyatt (The Farmer)

Chester Wyatt (The Farmer)

From the very early stages of development, the farmer was conceived as a comedic character. The main challenge was defining what kind of humor he should embody: after several considerations, he was ultimately designed as a naive and clumsy figure, more distinctive and spontaneous than the classic grumpy farmer archetype. These traits are reflected in his appearance: a pronounced nose, drooping eyes, and a slightly goofy smile with two protruding teeth and a perpetually distracted expression. He is tall and slim, yet slightly hunched and unkempt, with slow, uncoordinated movements that emphasize his lazy demeanor. His color palette and outfit complete his personality: a faded, oversized denim overall, a white shirt with orange details, and an old, worn-out hat.


Reginald Tucker Carrington Montgomery (The Mayor)

Reginald Tucker Carrington Montgomery (The Mayor)

In designing the mayor’s character, the goal was to create a clear contrast with the farmer, both physically and in personality, to make their dynamic immediately recognizable.
The mayor is short, stocky, and stiff in his movements, with a formal and authoritative attitude that contrasts sharply with the farmer’s spontaneous clumsiness. He is proud, serious, and convinced of his own importance, but this excessive composure makes him comical, as reality constantly undercuts his self-image.
Visually, he stands out through his neat clothing and cool-toned color palette (magenta, purple, and blue), in opposition to the warm tones of the rural characters. This visually isolates him from his surroundings, emphasizing his out-of-place nature.
The mayor thus embodies the archetype of the overly serious character who, without meaning to, becomes ridiculous—perfectly contributing to the short film’s ironic and surreal tone.

 

Cornelius Wyatt The IV (The Chicken)

Cornelius Wyatt The IV
(The Chicken)

The creation of the chicken character, Cornelius, was one of the most stimulating challenges of the project. The goal was to combine his narrative purpose with strong comedic potential, making him the farmer’s comic sidekick. After studying various chicken breeds, the team chose a design that balanced agility and clumsiness, reflecting his dual nature: naive and awkward, yet clever and observant.
Physically, Cornelius has a compact, rounded build, perfect for physical gags, yet still agile. His warm color palette makes him lively and instantly recognizable.
The character was modeled in Maya and sculpted in ZBrush, with special attention to detail and the needs of the rigger. During the texturing phase, bold color gradients were tested. Cornelius ultimately became a key element of the short film, bringing energy, humor, and rhythm to the story.

 

 

ENVIRONMENTS

Living room

Living room

The living room appears as a chaotic space, filled with eccentric items and knick-knacks that perfectly reflect the protagonist’s personality. Among the details, there are posters that reveal his musical passions, quirky objects such as a rubber chicken, a SNES console used during his leisure time, and numerous items scattered out of place.
From a technical perspective, the scene features intentionally stylized forms in the main pieces of furniture, such as the armchair, the cabinet, and the television, complemented by secondary objects, including a remote control, a stove, and a gramophone in the background.


Desk

Desk

The desk serves as the protagonist’s command center, the place where all his passions and obsessions come together. Among them stand out baseball, but above all, his fixation with aliens.
On the wall behind him hangs a bulletin board filled with photographs, clues, and newspaper clippings about UFO sightings, undeniable evidence of the existence of extraterrestrial beings.
A particularly striking detail is the computer: an old case held together with duct tape, powered by a car battery, and topped with an improvised antenna in a desperate attempt to pick up a signal.

 

Kitchen

Kitchen

Bathed in the warm light of the morning sun, the kitchen initially conveys a sense of calm and comfort — the perfect setting for a quiet breakfast. Yet, a closer look reveals the unmistakable touch of the protagonist and his chaotic lifestyle: socks stuffed into pots, a sink overflowing with dirty dishes, scattered utensils, grimy rags draped carelessly around, and even a sausage poking out of the refrigerator.
Despite the mess, this cluttered environment perfectly mirrors his eccentric personality, turning disorder into character. Adding a humorous twist, a bowl of chicken feed sits on the floor with a handwritten sign reading “Corny food” — a playful detail that reinforces the quirky charm of the space and the ever-present hint of absurdity that defines the protagonist’s world.

Entrance

Entrance

The entrance, bathed in sunlight that cuts dramatically across the scene, welcomes the viewer with a warm and lively atmosphere. The staircase immediately draws attention with a wall completely covered in framed pictures, each one telling a small part of the protagonists’ story. At the heart of the scene stands the main door with a tiny door just for the chicken.


Bedroom

Bedroom

The bedroom is a small, intimate space where some of the story’s most meaningful elements come together. On the protagonist’s nightstand rests the nest of his inseparable pet, who insists on sleeping close to its owner despite knowing what awaits each morning: becoming his “living alarm clock,” with all the inevitable consequences of trying to wake someone lazy.


Exterior

Exterior

The exterior setting of the short film portrays a quiet American farmhouse, with the protagonist’s home at its center, a structure designed to mirror his personality. The surrounding details bring the scene to life: a small chicken coop, a mailbox topped with an antenna and covered in alien stickers, and rustic touches like barrels, hay bales, and wooden fences.
Completing this quirky setting is the farmer’s stall, a small, improvised structure built from reused materials. With a faded beach umbrella, an old mini fridge, and a rusty bicycle, it gives off a feeling of creative messiness.
